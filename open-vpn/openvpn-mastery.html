Chapter 00

The Problem the Internet Never Solved

Before you understand the solution, you must feel the pain of the problem.

The internet was designed in the 1970s for one purpose: resilient communication between trusted academic institutions. Security was an afterthought. The original architects never imagined billions of strangers, hostile governments, corporations mining data, or remote workers connecting to sensitive systems from coffee shops.

Every packet you send across the public internet is, by default, naked. It travels through routers, ISPs, and infrastructure you do not own or trust. Anyone with access to those hops — your ISP, a government, an attacker on the same café Wi-Fi — can read it, modify it, or silently record it.

[ Your Laptop ] ──→ [ ISP Router ] ──→ [ Backbone Router ] ──→ [ Remote ISP ] ──→ [ Server ] ↑ ↑ ↑ Anyone here can intercept your packets in plain sight

The internet is not a private telephone line between you and the server. It is closer to shouting across a crowded room. Everyone in between can hear you. SSL/TLS protects the content, but your IP, your destination, your traffic patterns — all visible.

The Three Core Problems VPNs Solve

1. Privacy: "Who's watching me?"

Your ISP sees every domain you visit, every connection you make. They sell this data. Governments demand it. Advertisers exploit it. A VPN wraps all your traffic in an encrypted tunnel so your ISP only sees: "This person is connected to a VPN server." Nothing more.

2. Security on Untrusted Networks

When you connect to a café Wi-Fi, you share the same local network with strangers. An attacker can perform ARP spoofing to pretend they're the router, intercepting all your traffic. A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel that bypasses this entirely — the attacker sees encrypted noise.

3. Remote Access: "The Network Extension Problem"

A company has servers on a private internal network (192.168.1.0/24). A remote employee needs to access those servers from home. Without a VPN, those servers are not reachable from the public internet at all — nor should they be. A VPN makes the employee's machine virtually join the private network, even from thousands of miles away.

This third use case — remote network access — is the primary reason enterprises run OpenVPN. Privacy is a bonus. The core engineering problem is: how do you make a machine that is physically somewhere else appear to be inside your private network?

What Makes OpenVPN Different from "Just Using HTTPS"?

HTTPS encrypts the content of your communication with one specific server. OpenVPN encrypts all your network traffic at the IP layer and routes it through a trusted tunnel endpoint. They solve different problems at different layers of the stack.

ProtectionHTTPSOpenVPN
Content encryptionYesYes
Hides destination IPNoYes
Protects all appsNoYes
Joins private networksNoYes
Works at layerL7 AppL3 Network
Chapter 01

The OSI Stack — Read It Like a Pro

Most engineers can recite the 7 layers. Almost none can tell you where the magic happens.

You know the OSI model. Let's read it differently — not as a list to memorize, but as a packaging system for data. Every layer wraps the data from the layer above it with its own header, creating a nesting doll of information called an encapsulation stack.

┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ L7 APPLICATION HTTP, DNS, SMTP — your actual data │ │ L6 PRESENTATION Encoding, encryption (TLS lives here)│ │ L5 SESSION Connection lifecycle management │ │ L4 TRANSPORT TCP/UDP — port numbers, reliability │ │ L3 NETWORK ◄── IP addresses, routing — VPN IS HERE │ │ L2 DATA LINK MAC addresses, Ethernet frames │ │ L1 PHYSICAL Actual bits on wire/wifi │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

OpenVPN intercepts at Layer 3 (IP). It takes raw IP packets destined for the outside world, encrypts them, and re-wraps them as UDP/TCP packets to send to the VPN server. Your packet gets a new IP header — like putting a sealed letter inside another envelope addressed to a PO Box. The postman only sees the outer envelope.

IP Addresses — The Two Worlds

There are two classes of IP addresses you must deeply understand:

Public IPs — The Internet World

Globally unique. Routable across the internet. Assigned by ISPs. Every packet on the public internet has a real, unique source and destination public IP. There are about 4.3 billion IPv4 addresses, and we ran out.

Private IPs — The Intranet World

Three ranges are reserved for private networks only. They are not routable on the public internet — no router on the backbone will forward a packet with a private destination IP. 

# Private IP ranges (RFC 1918)
10.0.0.0/8       # 10.x.x.x — large enterprises, usually
172.16.0.0/12    # 172.16.x.x to 172.31.x.x
192.168.0.0/16   # 192.168.x.x — your home router

OpenVPN typically assigns clients a private IP in a dedicated subnet (like 10.8.0.0/24). When you see that 10.x.x.x address on your VPN client — that IS your virtual network identity inside the tunnel. This is the IP that private servers on the other end see when you connect.

CIDR Notation — Stop Guessing, Start Calculating

The slash notation (like /24) tells you how many bits of the address are the network portion. The remaining bits are for hosts. 

# 10.8.0.0/24
# 24 bits = network, 8 bits = hosts
# 2^8 = 256 addresses, minus 2 (network + broadcast) = 254 usable

Network:   10.8.0.0      # the network itself
Gateway:   10.8.0.1      # typically the VPN server
Clients:   10.8.0.2 - 10.8.0.254
Broadcast: 10.8.0.255    # can't assign to any host

NAT — The Great Address Translator

Network Address Translation is why your home network can have 50 devices all sharing one public IP. Your router rewrites the source IP of every outgoing packet from the private 192.168.x.x address to the public IP, and keeps a translation table to route replies back.

Your PC: 192.168.1.10:54321 │ ▼ NAT ROUTER: rewrites source to public IP Internet packet: 203.0.113.5:54321 ──→ destination │ Reply comes back to 203.0.113.5:54321 Router looks up table: 54321 → forward to 192.168.1.10

This is why "port forwarding" exists. When a connection originates from the internet toward your public IP, the NAT router has no translation table entry — it doesn't know where to send it. You have to explicitly tell it: "port 1194 incoming → forward to 192.168.1.50". This is exactly what you do when exposing an OpenVPN server behind a home router.

TCP vs UDP — Choose Wisely

OpenVPN can run over either TCP or UDP. Understanding why this matters changes how you architect VPN setups.

PropertyTCPUDP
Guarantees deliveryYes — retransmitsNo — fire and forget
OrderingGuaranteedOut of order possible
SpeedSlower — more overheadFaster
VPN recommendationFallback onlyPreferred (port 1194)

TCP over TCP Problem: If you tunnel TCP traffic inside OpenVPN running over TCP, you get double retransmission. When the outer TCP layer retransmits, the inner TCP doesn't know — it also retransmits. This creates catastrophic performance degradation. This is why OpenVPN's default is UDP. If you must use TCP (to bypass firewalls on port 443), you pay a real performance price.

Chapter 02

Cryptography — The Engine Under the Hood

You don't need to implement it. You need to understand it deeply enough to configure it correctly and know when it fails.

OpenVPN is powered by OpenSSL — the same library that powers HTTPS. Understanding the crypto stack is what separates engineers who configure VPNs by copying examples from those who can audit, troubleshoot, and architect secure systems.

Symmetric Encryption — The Fast Lock

You have one key. It encrypts and decrypts. It's extremely fast — hardware-accelerated on modern CPUs (AES-NI instruction set). This is what encrypts your data stream inside the VPN tunnel. 

# AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)
# Block size: 128 bits
# Key sizes: 128, 192, or 256 bits

# OpenVPN recommendation:
cipher AES-256-GCM   # Gold standard as of OpenVPN 2.4+

# GCM = Galois/Counter Mode
# It's an AEAD cipher — provides BOTH encryption AND authentication
# in one operation. Replaces the need for a separate HMAC.

AES-256-GCM is called an AEAD (Authenticated Encryption with Associated Data) cipher. It doesn't just encrypt your data — it also mathematically proves that the data hasn't been tampered with in transit. One algorithm doing two jobs that used to require two separate steps. This is why older configs had both a cipher and an auth directive — GCM made the auth redundant.

Asymmetric Encryption — The Key Exchange

You have two keys: a public key (share with everyone) and a private key (never share). Data encrypted with one can only be decrypted with the other. Critically, knowing the public key does not let you reverse-engineer the private key (mathematically infeasible with RSA-2048+).

The problem: this is thousands of times slower than symmetric encryption. You can't use it for bulk data.

The solution: use asymmetric crypto to securely exchange a symmetric key, then use symmetric crypto for everything else. This hybrid approach is exactly how TLS works — and exactly how OpenVPN's handshake works.

CLIENT SERVER │ │ │──── "Hello, here's my public key" ──→│ │ Server verifies│ cert against CA │←── "Here's mine, it's signed by CA" ─│ │ │ │═══ Negotiate shared session key ═════│ ← asymmetric math │ │ │═══ All data now encrypted with ══════│ ← symmetric AES │ that session key │

Hashing — The Fingerprint System

A hash function takes any input and produces a fixed-size output (e.g., 256 bits for SHA-256). It's a one-way function — you can't reverse it. The same input always produces the same output, but changing even one bit changes the entire output unpredictably.

  • Integrity — Proves data wasn't tampered with in transit (HMAC)
  • Certificate fingerprints — Verify a certificate is authentic
  • Password storage — Store hash, never the password itself
  • Key derivation — Generate encryption keys from passwords (PBKDF2)

OpenVPN's tls-auth directive adds an HMAC layer to ALL TLS handshake packets. This means an attacker who sends a packet to your VPN port without the correct HMAC key gets zero response. Your server doesn't even begin the TLS handshake. This hides your VPN from scanners and blocks entire classes of DoS attacks before they reach the crypto layer. Most admins skip this. Titans don't.

Diffie-Hellman — The Genius of Public Key Exchange

Here's the beautiful problem: if Alice and Bob have never met and want to share a secret key, how do they do it over a channel that an eavesdropper can see?

Diffie-Hellman solves this with a mathematical magic trick. Both parties exchange public numbers. Through modular exponentiation, they each independently arrive at the same secret number without ever transmitting that secret — and an eavesdropper who saw all the exchanged numbers still cannot compute it.

The "key exchange" in the name is slightly misleading. No key is actually transmitted. Both sides compute the same key independently from public information. It's like mixing paint: I send you yellow, you send me blue, we both mix them with our private color (red), and we both get brown — but the person watching saw yellow and blue and can't figure out our private red or the final brown. This is the foundation of all secure internet communication.

Modern OpenVPN uses ECDH (Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman) — same mathematical idea but using elliptic curves, which achieve the same security with much smaller key sizes and faster computation. This is why modern configs show dh none and use ecdh-curve prime256v1 instead of large DH parameter files.

Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS)

This is one of the most important concepts in modern security that most engineers cannot explain.

Without PFS: if an attacker records all your encrypted traffic today, and steals your server's private key in the future, they can decrypt all past traffic retroactively.

With PFS: each session generates a temporary, ephemeral key. Even with the server's long-term private key, past sessions cannot be decrypted because those ephemeral keys were never stored anywhere.

OpenVPN with TLS mode automatically gets PFS because TLS 1.2+ uses ephemeral Diffie-Hellman (DHE/ECDHE) ciphersuites. Each connection negotiates its own throwaway key. Past sessions become cryptographically sealed even from the server itself. This is why the NSA's BULLRUN program focused on attacking key exchange implementations rather than AES — the encryption itself is solid.

Chapter 03

TUN / TAP — The Kernel's Virtual Interface

This is the piece almost no tutorial explains. It is the heart of how VPNs physically work at the OS level.

When you run OpenVPN, the OS creates a new virtual network interface — you'll see it with ip addr or ifconfig. It looks exactly like a physical NIC, but it has no hardware. It's a software-defined network interface implemented in the kernel.

How a Normal Network Interface Works

Application writes to socket ↓ Kernel network stack processes it (IP/TCP headers) ↓ Physical NIC driver sends bits over wire

How a TUN Interface Works

Application writes to socket (e.g., curl) ↓ Kernel network stack builds IP packet ↓ Packet goes to TUN device (tun0) — no hardware ↓ OpenVPN process reads from /dev/tun0 — like reading a file! ↓ OpenVPN encrypts the IP packet ↓ Sends encrypted packet as UDP to VPN server

The TUN device is a pipe between the kernel's network stack and a userspace process. When the kernel wants to send a packet out through tun0, OpenVPN reads it. When OpenVPN writes to tun0, the kernel processes it as if it arrived from a network. This is the virtual machine trick applied to networking — the kernel is "tricked" into thinking tun0 is a real network card.

TUN vs TAP — Layer 3 vs Layer 2

FeatureTUNTAP
OSI LayerLayer 3 — IP packetsLayer 2 — Ethernet frames
CarriesIP packets onlyFull Ethernet frames (IP, ARP, etc.)
Use caseRoute-based VPN (most common)Bridge-based VPN (network extension)
PerformanceHigherLower (more overhead)
OpenVPN defaultYes — dev tunSpecific use cases only

Use dev tap when you need to extend a Layer 2 network — for example, to join a remote machine to the same broadcast domain as your office LAN so it can receive DHCP from the office DHCP server, or run protocols that rely on broadcast/ARP. Use dev tun (routing mode) for everything else. TAP mode is more complex and has a real performance cost.

The Packet's Full Journey Through OpenVPN

CLIENT (10.8.0.6) SERVER (10.8.0.1) curl http://192.168.1.50/api ↓ Kernel: build IP packet src: 10.8.0.6 dst: 192.168.1.50 ↓ Route table: 192.168.1.0/24 → tun0 ↓ OpenVPN reads from tun0 Encrypts: AES-256-GCM Adds HMAC ↓ New outer packet: src: client-public-IP dst: server-public-IP:1194 payload: [ENCRYPTED ORIGINAL PACKET] ↓ ════════ Public Internet ════════ ↓ Server OpenVPN receives UDP packet Decrypts payload Gets original: src 10.8.0.6 → dst 192.168.1.50 ↓ Writes to tun0 on server Kernel routes to 192.168.1.0/24 via eth0 ↓ Reaches the target server!
Chapter 04

PKI — The Trust Hierarchy

This is where most VPN setups get broken. Understand this, and you become the person everyone asks when certificates go wrong.

Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) is the system of certificates, signing authorities, and trust chains that answers one fundamental question: "How do I know I'm talking to who I think I'm talking to?"

A certificate is a digital document that binds a public key to an identity (a domain name, an IP, an organization name). But anyone can create a certificate. The trick is getting it signed by someone the other party trusts.

The Certificate Trust Chain

┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ ROOT CA (Certificate Auth) │ ← Self-signed. The "god" of the chain. │ ca.crt / ca.key │ Everyone must trust this explicitly. └──────────┬──────────────────┘ │ signs ▼ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ SERVER CERTIFICATE │ ← Signed by CA. Proves "I am the VPN server." │ server.crt / server.key │ └─────────────────────────────┘ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ CLIENT CERTIFICATES │ ← Signed by same CA. Proves "I am a trusted client." │ client1.crt / client1.key │ Every user gets their own cert. └─────────────────────────────┘

With OpenVPN's mutual TLS, BOTH sides present certificates signed by the same CA. The client verifies the server's cert (prevents connecting to a rogue server). The server verifies the client's cert (replaces or supplements username/password auth). If a client's cert is compromised, you revoke just that cert — you don't need to change anything else. This is elegance in security design.

EasyRSA — The PKI Toolchain

OpenVPN's companion tool EasyRSA creates and manages your private CA. Key files you'll work with: 

# Your CA infrastructure
ca.crt         # CA public certificate — distribute to ALL clients and server
ca.key         # CA private key — KEEP THIS LOCKED DOWN. Never on server.

# Server files
server.crt     # Server certificate (signed by CA)
server.key     # Server private key
dh.pem        # (Legacy) Diffie-Hellman params — use 'dh none' in modern configs

# Per-client files
client1.crt    # Client certificate
client1.key    # Client private key
ta.key         # TLS Authentication key — shared secret, extra HMAC layer

The ca.key (your root CA private key) should NEVER live on the VPN server. Generate it on an air-gapped machine, sign certificates as needed, then destroy or vault the key. If your VPN server is compromised and the attacker gets ca.key, they can issue trusted certificates to impersonate any client. Keep the CA key offline — this is the practice used by major CAs like Let's Encrypt.

Certificate Revocation List (CRL)

When an employee leaves, or a client device is stolen, you need to invalidate their certificate without touching anything else. The CRL is a signed list of revoked certificate serial numbers. OpenVPN checks this list on every new connection. 

# Generate a CRL
./easyrsa gen-crl

# In server.conf
crl-verify /etc/openvpn/crl.pem

# Revoke a specific client
./easyrsa revoke client1
./easyrsa gen-crl
# Copy new crl.pem to server — takes effect immediately

Certificate Attributes That Matter

OpenVPN uses X.509 certificate extensions to enforce roles. The key one: nsCertType and extendedKeyUsage. These prevent a client certificate from being used as a server certificate (and vice versa) — this blocks a class of attack called certificate spoofing. 

# In server.conf, add these checks:
remote-cert-tls client    # Server verifies connecting cert is a CLIENT cert

# In client.conf, add:
remote-cert-tls server    # Client verifies server cert is a SERVER cert

Without remote-cert-tls, a stolen client certificate could theoretically be used to set up a rogue server that clients trust. Always add this directive. It costs nothing and prevents a real attack vector that most tutorials never mention.

Chapter 05

Routing — The Brain of the Network

Understanding routing is understanding how the internet makes decisions. This is where most VPN configurations break.

A routing table is the map the kernel uses to decide: "For a packet destined to IP address X — which interface do I send it out through, and what's the next hop?" 

# View your routing table
ip route show

# Typical output on a VPN client:
default       via 192.168.1.1 dev eth0      # → Everything else → home router
10.8.0.0/24   via 10.8.0.1   dev tun0      # → VPN subnet → through tunnel
192.168.1.0/24               dev eth0      # → Local LAN → direct on eth0

Full Tunnel vs Split Tunnel — The Architectural Choice

Full Tunnel Mode (redirect-gateway)

ALL traffic — including internet browsing — goes through the VPN server. Your ISP sees only encrypted traffic to the VPN server. The VPN server's IP is what websites see. 

# In server.conf
push "redirect-gateway def1 bypass-dhcp"
push "dhcp-option DNS 1.1.1.1"

Split Tunnel Mode

Only specific subnets go through the VPN tunnel. Internet traffic goes directly through the local connection. Faster, but the VPN server doesn't see your general browsing. 

# In server.conf — push only specific routes
push "route 192.168.10.0 255.255.255.0"   # Only internal office subnet
push "route 10.0.0.0 255.0.0.0"           # Only private 10.x range

The def1 flag in redirect-gateway is elegant engineering. Instead of replacing the default route (which would break the VPN connection itself — the VPN server would suddenly be unreachable!), it adds two more specific routes: 0.0.0.0/1 and 128.0.0.0/1. These two cover the entire IP space but are more specific than 0.0.0.0/0. The routing table always prefers more specific routes — so these "win" without touching the original default route. The original route still handles the VPN server's own IP.

IP Forwarding — The Gate That Must Be Opened

By default, a Linux machine drops packets that aren't destined for itself. To act as a router (which your VPN server must), you need IP forwarding enabled. 

# Temporary (lost on reboot)
echo 1 > /proc/sys/net/ipv4/ip_forward

# Permanent — edit /etc/sysctl.conf
net.ipv4.ip_forward = 1
sysctl -p

Forgetting to enable IP forwarding is the single most common reason a "working" OpenVPN connection can't reach anything beyond the VPN server's own IP. The tunnel is established, pings reach 10.8.0.1, but nothing further responds. Check ip_forward first.

NAT on the VPN Server — Masquerading

When VPN clients (10.8.0.0/24) want to reach internet resources through the server, you need NAT on the server's outgoing interface — otherwise replies would be sent back to a 10.8.x.x address, which is private and unreachable from the internet. 

# Using iptables — the VPN server NATs client traffic onto its public interface
iptables -t nat -A POSTROUTING -s 10.8.0.0/24 -o eth0 -j MASQUERADE

# Using nftables (modern systems)
nft add rule ip nat postrouting ip saddr 10.8.0.0/24 oif eth0 masquerade

Client-Specific Routing (CCD)

OpenVPN's Client Config Directory (CCD) lets you assign a fixed IP to a specific client, or push routes to specific clients only. This is powerful for enterprise setups. 

# In server.conf
client-config-dir /etc/openvpn/ccd
route 192.168.100.0 255.255.255.0   # Tell server about this subnet

# /etc/openvpn/ccd/client1  (filename = CN of client's certificate)
ifconfig-push 10.8.0.10 10.8.0.9   # Assign fixed IP to this client
iroute 192.168.100.0 255.255.255.0  # Client sits in front of this subnet

The iroute directive is internal routing inside OpenVPN's own routing logic. The route in server.conf is for the kernel's routing table. You need BOTH to route traffic to a subnet that exists behind a VPN client. This "iroute vs route" distinction breaks almost every site-to-site VPN setup attempt until you understand it.

Chapter 06

OpenVPN — Architecture Deep Dive

Now that you have the foundations, OpenVPN clicks into place perfectly.

OpenVPN was created by James Yonan in 2001. It's open-source, runs in userspace (no kernel module required), and uses SSL/TLS for its security layer. It is not IPsec, not WireGuard, not PPTP. It is its own VPN protocol built on top of OpenSSL.

The Two Operating Modes

Point-to-Point (Legacy)

One server, one client. Pre-shared key authentication. Simple but doesn't scale. Used for linking two routers.

TLS Mode (Standard)

Full PKI infrastructure. One server, many clients. Each client has a certificate. This is what all modern deployments use.

The Connection Handshake — Step by Step

  1. Client connects to server on UDP port 1194. If tls-auth is set, the packet has an HMAC. Server verifies it immediately — if invalid, silently drops.
  2. TLS handshake begins. Client and server exchange certificates. Both verify against the CA.
  3. DH/ECDH key exchange occurs. A shared master secret is derived. Per-session symmetric keys are generated from it.
  4. Control channel is now encrypted. Further messages over the control channel are protected with these session keys.
  5. Server pushes configuration to client: IP address, routes, DNS servers via push directives.
  6. TUN/TAP interface is brought up on the client. Routes are added to the kernel routing table.
  7. Data channel is active. All subsequent VPN traffic uses a separate, high-performance encrypted data channel.

OpenVPN uses two separate channels: a control channel (for handshake, renegotiation, keepalives) and a data channel (for actual traffic). They can use different cipher suites optimized for their respective purposes. The data channel uses the fast AEAD cipher. The control channel can use more careful TLS. This separation is a key design insight.

Annotated Server Configuration

# /etc/openvpn/server.conf — Annotated Modern Config

port 1194
proto udp        # Use UDP for performance
dev tun          # Layer 3 routing mode

# Certificates
ca   /etc/openvpn/ca.crt
cert /etc/openvpn/server.crt
key  /etc/openvpn/server.key
dh   none         # Use ECDH instead of DH params file

# VPN subnet — clients get IPs from here
server 10.8.0.0 255.255.255.0

# Persist client IP assignments between restarts
ifconfig-pool-persist /var/log/openvpn/ipp.txt

# Push routes to clients
push "route 192.168.1.0 255.255.255.0"  # Internal office subnet
push "dhcp-option DNS 10.8.0.1"          # Use VPN server for DNS

# Security hardening
tls-auth /etc/openvpn/ta.key 0         # 0=server, 1=client
cipher AES-256-GCM
tls-version-min 1.2
tls-cipher TLS-ECDHE-RSA-WITH-AES-256-GCM-SHA384
remote-cert-tls client                   # Require proper client cert type

# Privilege dropping — run as nobody after startup
user nobody
group nogroup

# Persist TUN device and keys across restarts
persist-key
persist-tun

# Keepalive: ping every 10s, restart if no ping for 120s
keepalive 10 120

# Revocation list
crl-verify /etc/openvpn/crl.pem

# Log verbosity (0-9, 3 is good for production)
verb 3

# Allow multiple clients to share one certificate (for testing only!)
# duplicate-cn

# Run a script when client connects/disconnects
client-connect    /etc/openvpn/scripts/on-connect.sh
client-disconnect /etc/openvpn/scripts/on-disconnect.sh

status /var/log/openvpn/status.log
log-append /var/log/openvpn/openvpn.log

The user nobody / group nogroup directives are critical. OpenVPN needs root to create the TUN device and modify routing tables. After that's done at startup, it drops privileges to a non-privileged user. This is defense-in-depth: even if OpenVPN is exploited, the attacker doesn't get root. Most tutorial configs never include this.

Chapter 07

Advanced Patterns — Level Up

These are the topics that separate the engineers who set up VPNs from those who design them.

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) with OpenVPN

OpenVPN supports a two-factor approach: certificate + password via PAM (Pluggable Authentication Module). Or certificate + TOTP (Google Authenticator). The client presents the TOTP code as the password field. 

# server.conf
plugin /usr/lib/openvpn/openvpn-plugin-auth-pam.so openvpn
client-cert-not-required  # Optional: cert OR password, not both
# Or require BOTH cert AND password:
username-as-common-name

Split DNS — The Overlooked Must-Have

In split tunnel mode, you typically want internal hostnames (db.internal, app.corp) to resolve via the corporate DNS server, while public domains use the local DNS. Split DNS sends only internal queries through the VPN DNS. 

# Push DNS server AND domain
push "dhcp-option DNS 10.8.0.1"
push "dhcp-option DOMAIN corp.internal"
# Client will use VPN DNS for *.corp.internal queries

High Availability — Active-Passive Failover

Production VPN cannot have a single point of failure. Standard pattern:

  1. Two OpenVPN servers share the same CA and configuration
  2. Both are behind a load balancer (HAProxy, AWS NLB) on the same VIP (Virtual IP)
  3. Health checks monitor each server; traffic fails over in <30 seconds
  4. Client configs list multiple remote directives — tries each in order
# client.conf — multiple server entries
remote vpn1.company.com 1194 udp
remote vpn2.company.com 1194 udp
remote-random          # Randomize order for load distribution
resolv-retry infinite
connect-retry 5 60

OpenVPN Over TCP Port 443 — Firewall Bypass

Some corporate or hotel firewalls block all UDP and all non-standard TCP ports. By running OpenVPN over TCP port 443 (HTTPS port), it becomes nearly impossible to block without breaking HTTPS entirely. 

# server.conf
proto tcp
port 443

# client.conf
remote vpn.company.com 443 tcp

A more elegant approach: run sslh (a port multiplexer) on port 443 that distinguishes real HTTPS traffic from OpenVPN TCP traffic and forwards each to the right service. Your HTTPS web server and OpenVPN both share port 443. This is the approach used when the server must also serve a website on port 443.

OpenVPN + Docker / Kubernetes

OpenVPN runs excellently in Docker. The key challenge: the container needs NET_ADMIN capability to create TUN devices and modify routes. 

# docker-compose.yml
services:
  openvpn:
    image: kylemanna/openvpn
    cap_add:
      - NET_ADMIN
    devices:
      - /dev/net/tun
    ports:
      - "1194:1194/udp"
    volumes:
      - ./openvpn-data:/etc/openvpn

Monitoring and Observability

A VPN you can't observe is a VPN you can't trust. 

# Enable the management interface
management 127.0.0.1 7505

# Connect to it:
nc 127.0.0.1 7505
# Commands: status, kill <client>, bytecount 5, log 20

# Or use the status file
status /var/log/openvpn/status.log 10  # Refresh every 10 seconds

Integrate OpenVPN status with Prometheus + Grafana using openvpn_exporter. Track: connected clients count, bytes in/out per client, connection duration, failed auth attempts. Set alerts on sudden drops in connections (server crash?) or spikes in failed auths (brute force?). This is production-grade VPN ops.

WireGuard is faster and simpler, but it has no native certificate-based auth, no built-in user management, and its kernel integration makes it less flexible to deploy in containers. OpenVPN's age and complexity is also its strength: every edge case has been solved, every bug found. For enterprise deployments with certificate management, user revocation, and complex routing needs, OpenVPN remains the gold standard.

Zero Trust vs VPN — The Future

The industry is moving toward Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA). Traditional VPNs grant network access — once inside, you can reach many resources. Zero Trust grants application-level access — you get access to exactly one app, not the network it sits in.

OpenVPN's commercial product (OpenVPN Access Server) has moved toward this model. But understanding OpenVPN gives you the foundations to understand ZTNA — it's the same crypto, the same certificate model, the same routing concepts, applied at a finer-grained level.

The 10 Things Titans Know That Others Don't

  1. tls-auth drops packets at the HMAC layer before TLS even starts — completely invisible to attackers.
  2. The "def1" trick — why redirect-gateway doesn't break the VPN connection to the server itself.
  3. iroute vs route — you need both to route traffic to subnets behind a client in site-to-site setups.
  4. TCP over TCP performance — why UDP is mandatory for tunnel transports carrying TCP traffic.
  5. ca.key must never be on the server — air-gap your CA, sign certs offline.
  6. AES-256-GCM is AEAD — it replaces both the cipher and the auth HMAC in legacy configs.
  7. Perfect Forward Secrecy — historical traffic remains secure even after private key compromise.
  8. user nobody / group nogroup — privilege dropping after startup. Defense in depth.
  9. remote-cert-tls — prevents certificate type spoofing (client cert used as server, vice versa).
  10. Split DNS — internal DNS only for internal domains; avoid routing all DNS queries through VPN.

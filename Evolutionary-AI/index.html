01 — Foundation

The Thinking Behind It

Before a single line of code, let's ask the question that motivated everything: How does nature solve impossibly hard problems without any instructions?

A bird's wing is aerodynamically near-perfect. A bacteria's flagellum is a marvel of nano-engineering. DNA error-correction mechanisms are more robust than most software. Nobody designed these — they emerged, through billions of iterations of a brutally simple process.

🔑
The Core Insight: You don't need to know HOW to build the best solution. You just need to know how to judge one. Given a way to score solutions, evolution will find them for you.

The Traditional Way

You define an exact procedure. Every step is explicit. The computer does exactly what you tell it — no more. The quality of the output is bounded by the quality of your instructions.

Works well when you already understand the problem deeply enough to specify the solution path.

The Evolutionary Way

You define what a good result looks like (a scoring function). The algorithm explores, recombines, and refines candidate solutions autonomously across many generations.

Works brilliantly when the search space is vast and the solution path is unknown or unknowable.

Darwin's Algorithm, Distilled

In 1859, Darwin described a mechanism so simple it fits in four lines — yet powerful enough to explain all of life's complexity:

01 — VARIATION

Individuals in a population are different from each other. No two are identical.

02 — HEREDITY

Offspring resemble their parents. Traits are passed down through generations.

03 — SELECTION

Individuals better suited to the environment leave more offspring. The rest fade away.

04 — TIME

Given enough iterations, complex adaptations emerge from nothing but these three rules.

💡
A Genetic Algorithm is a direct computer implementation of Darwin's mechanism — but instead of organisms adapting to nature, we have candidate solutions adapting to your problem.

What Problems Actually Need This?

GA shines when the search space is too large for exhaustive search, when the problem has no clean mathematical derivative, or when you need "good enough" rather than provably optimal.

COMBINATORIAL

Route planning, scheduling, bin packing — problems where you're choosing from an astronomically large set of arrangements.

DESIGN SPACE

Engineering shapes, neural architectures, circuit layouts — where the solution is a structure, not a number.

MULTI-OBJECTIVE

Optimise cost and performance and reliability simultaneously — trade-off problems with no single right answer.

02 — Context

GA vs Other AI Approaches

Different AI techniques are different search strategies. Understanding when to reach for GA (versus gradient descent, exhaustive search, or reinforcement learning) comes from understanding what each one assumes about the problem.

The Fitness Landscape Analogy

Imagine every possible solution to your problem laid out as a landscape. Height = how good the solution is. Finding the best solution means finding the highest peak.

Gradient Descent is like a blindfolded hiker — they can only feel the slope under their feet and walk uphill. They'll reach a peak, but not necessarily the peak.

A Genetic Algorithm releases an entire population of hikers scattered across the landscape. They share information, combine their best positions, and occasionally take random jumps — dramatically increasing the odds of finding the true global maximum.

🏔️
Local vs Global Optima — this is the fundamental problem GA is designed to escape. When a gradient method gets trapped on a local hill, GA keeps the whole population alive to explore other valleys.
● Gradient Descent — gets trapped   ● GA Population — explores freely

The Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP)

A salesman must visit 15 cities exactly once and return home. What's the shortest route? This is NP-hard — the number of possible routes is 15! = 1.3 trillion.

BRUTE FORCE

Check every permutation. Guaranteed optimal. But with 15 cities and 1 trillion routes — at a billion checks/second — you're waiting 21 minutes. With 25 cities? Longer than the age of the universe.

Time complexity: O(n!)
NEAREST NEIGHBOUR

Greedy heuristic: always visit the closest unvisited city. Fast, but typically produces routes 20-25% worse than optimal. Gets trapped in local decisions it can't undo.

Time complexity: O(n²)
GENETIC ALGORITHM

Encodes routes as chromosomes. Crossover swaps city segments between parents. Mutation swaps two cities. Consistently finds routes within 1-3% of optimal in seconds.

Time complexity: O(gen × pop × n)
🧭
Real logistics companies (FedEx, UPS, Amazon) use evolutionary algorithms for vehicle routing. UPS alone saves 100 million miles per year using route optimisation. The TSP is not an academic toy — it's a billion-dollar problem.

Neural Networks and GAs are both inspired by biology, but they attack problems from opposite directions.

REAL EXAMPLE: NEUROEVOLUTION

What if you use GA to evolve neural networks? This is called NeuroEvolution (NEAT algorithm). Instead of backpropagation, a GA evolves the network's topology and weights. OpenAI used this to evolve agents that play Atari games without any explicit reward shaping.

REAL EXAMPLE: NEURAL ARCHITECTURE SEARCH

Google's AutoML uses evolutionary algorithms to search the space of neural network architectures. The GA discovers architectures that outperform those hand-designed by expert researchers — including finding EfficientNet, one of the most accurate image classifiers ever built.

GA vs Reinforcement Learning

Both GA and Reinforcement Learning (RL) can train agents to behave intelligently — without labelled data. But the search strategy is fundamentally different.

RL APPROACH

One agent interacts with an environment over time. Receives rewards and penalties. Updates its policy incrementally using temporal difference learning. Sequential, correlated experience.

GA APPROACH

A population of policies (chromosomes) are evaluated in parallel. No credit assignment problem. No sparse reward issue. The fitness function evaluates complete episodes.

🎮
Real example: Uber AI Labs showed in 2017 that simple genetic algorithms matched or beat deep RL (PPO, A3C) on many MuJoCo locomotion tasks — while being 10× simpler to implement and massively parallelisable across CPUs.
WHEN TO CHOOSE GA OVER RL
  • Reward is sparse or delayed (no step-by-step feedback)
  • You can parallelise environment evaluations
  • The episode is short enough to evaluate completely
  • You want to avoid the hyperparameter sensitivity of deep RL

🧬 Encoding — The Chromosome

The first challenge is representation: how do you encode a candidate solution as a string of data that can be mixed and mutated? This encoding is called a chromosome.

The chromosome is the genome of your solution. Each position is a gene. The value at each position is an allele.

💡
The power of GA lies here: you can encode almost anything. A route, a weight, a schedule, a network architecture, a trading rule — anything reducible to a sequence of values becomes evolvable.

👥 Population

Instead of starting with one solution and improving it (as gradient descent does), GA starts with a population of many random solutions — typically 50 to 1000 chromosomes.

This is the core structural difference. You're not following one path through the search space. You're sampling many points simultaneously, which gives the algorithm a panoramic view of the landscape.

🌍
Think of it like sending out 100 explorers instead of one. Even if most explorers find mediocre territory, the chances of someone landing near a great region are dramatically higher.

Population size is a key trade-off: larger populations explore more broadly but cost more computation per generation.

📊 Fitness Function

The fitness function is the only thing you need to define. It takes a chromosome and returns a number: how good is this solution?

This is the most profound freedom GA gives you. The fitness function is a black box — it can call a physics simulation, run a game, query a database, measure protein folding energy, calculate sharpe ratio of a portfolio... anything.

⚡
No gradients. No derivatives. No mathematical constraints. If you can evaluate a solution, you can evolve toward better ones. This is why GA works on problems that completely break gradient methods.

🎯 Selection

Selection picks which individuals get to reproduce. The key principle: fitter individuals should reproduce more often — but not exclusively. If only the top 2 survive, diversity collapses.

ROULETTE WHEEL (Fitness Proportionate)

Each individual gets a slice of a wheel proportional to its fitness. Spin the wheel to select a parent. Fitter individuals have bigger slices — more likely to be chosen, but not guaranteed.

TOURNAMENT SELECTION

Pick k random individuals. The fittest among those k wins. Repeat for each parent slot. Simpler, controls selection pressure via k. Most widely used in practice.

🎲
Selection must retain some randomness. Pure elitism (always take the best) leads to premature convergence — the population becomes a clone army stuck on one local hill.

🔀 Crossover (Recombination)

Two parent chromosomes combine to produce offspring. A random crossover point is chosen — genes before the point come from Parent A, genes after come from Parent B (single-point crossover).

Here's the non-obvious magic: a child can score higher than both parents. Parent A might have great genes on the left half, Parent B on the right. Crossover can combine both advantages in one individual — something neither parent or their siblings could achieve alone.

☢️ Mutation

After crossover, each gene has a small probability (typically 0.1%–2%) of being randomly changed. This is mutation.

Mutation seems like random noise — and it is. But it's essential noise. Here's why:

Without mutation, the population can only ever recombine alleles already present in the initial population. If the optimal solution requires a gene value that nobody in your starting population has — you'll never find it. Mutation introduces genuinely new genetic material.

🎲
Mutation rate is the search temperature. Too high → random walk, no convergence. Too low → premature convergence. The sweet spot keeps diversity alive while still allowing fitness to improve.

The Full Algorithm Loop

All six mechanics combine into a simple iterative loop. This pseudocode is the complete algorithm:

INITIALISE population with random chromosomes
EVALUATE fitness of each individual
WHILE termination condition not met DO
SELECT parents via fitness-proportionate selection
APPLY crossover → generate offspring
APPLY mutation to offspring (low probability)
EVALUATE fitness of offspring
REPLACE population (elitism + new offspring)
INCREMENT generation counter
END WHILE
RETURN best individual found

05 — Real World

Where Genetic Algorithms Are Used Today

GA isn't a textbook curiosity. It's actively deployed in some of the world's most complex industrial and scientific systems.

✈️

Aerospace Engineering

NASA used GA to evolve the shape of a satellite antenna for a 2006 mission. The resulting design was bizarre-looking — nothing a human would sketch — but outperformed all human-designed alternatives. It fit in a smaller package and performed better.

🚚

Logistics & Routing

Vehicle routing, delivery scheduling, airline crew rostering — all combinatorial NP-hard problems where GA finds near-optimal solutions. UPS ORION routing system saves ~100 million miles/year using evolutionary optimisation.

💊

Drug Discovery

Evolving molecular structures toward desired binding properties. GA explores the chemical search space (estimated at 10⁶⁰ drug-like molecules) far more efficiently than exhaustive search. Used in lead compound optimisation.

📈

Quantitative Finance

Evolving trading rules, portfolio weights, and strategy parameters. GA handles non-differentiable objectives (like Sharpe ratio, drawdown constraints) that gradient methods can't touch. Used by hedge funds for strategy discovery.

🏗️

Structural Engineering

Optimising beam placement, material distribution (topology optimisation), and load-bearing structures. GA finds lightweight, strong designs that structural engineers manually couldn't explore. Used in car chassis and bridge design.

🧠

Neural Architecture Search

Google's AmoebaNet and AutoML systems use evolutionary algorithms to discover CNN architectures. The evolved architectures consistently match or outperform human-designed ones on ImageNet benchmarks.

🎮

Game AI & Simulation

Evolving NPC behaviour trees, level designs, and game balancing parameters. Neuroevolution (NEAT) evolved the first agents to play Mario, Doom, and complex strategy games without handcrafted rules.

⚡

Power Grid Optimisation

Scheduling power generation, load balancing, and transmission routing in electrical grids. GA handles the combinatorial complexity of multi-constraint, multi-objective power flow problems that require near-real-time solutions.

06 — Implementation

Libraries & Frameworks

You don't need to implement GA from scratch for production systems. These battle-tested libraries handle the evolutionary machinery — you just define the chromosome encoding and fitness function.

DEAP
Distributed Evolutionary Algorithms in Python
Python · Most Powerful

The gold standard for evolutionary computation research and industry. Supports GA, GP (genetic programming), ES, and multi-objective optimisation (NSGA-II). Highly flexible — you define everything.

from deap import base, creator, tools, algorithms
import random

# Define fitness: maximise a single objective
creator.create("FitnessMax", base.Fitness, weights=(1.0,))
creator.create("Individual", list, fitness=creator.FitnessMax)

toolbox = base.Toolbox()
toolbox.register("attr_bool", random.randint, 0, 1)
toolbox.register("individual", tools.initRepeat,
    creator.Individual, toolbox.attr_bool, n=100)
toolbox.register("population", tools.initRepeat,
    list, toolbox.individual)

# YOUR fitness function — the only thing you write
def eval_fitness(individual):
    return sum(individual),  # must return a tuple

toolbox.register("evaluate", eval_fitness)
toolbox.register("mate",    tools.cxTwoPoint)
toolbox.register("mutate",  tools.mutFlipBit, indpb=0.02)
toolbox.register("select",  tools.selTournament, tournsize=3)

pop = toolbox.population(n=300)
result = algorithms.eaSimple(pop, toolbox,
    cxpb=0.5, mutpb=0.2, ngen=40, verbose=True)

PyGAD
Python Genetic Algorithm Library
Python · Easiest Start

The most beginner-friendly GA library. Works directly with NumPy arrays and has built-in Keras/PyTorch integration for neuroevolution. Minimal boilerplate — just define the fitness function and call run().

import pygad

# Fitness function — receives solution + its index
def fitness_func(ga_instance, solution, sol_idx):
    output = sum(solution * [4, -2, 3.5, 5, -11, -4.7])
    fitness = 1.0 / (abs(output - 44) + 0.000001)
    return fitness

ga_instance = pygad.GA(
    num_generations     = 200,
    num_parents_mating  = 5,
    fitness_func        = fitness_func,
    sol_per_pop         = 8,
    num_genes           = 6,
    init_range_low      = -4,
    init_range_high     = 4,
    mutation_percent_genes = 10
)

ga_instance.run()
solution, fitness, idx = ga_instance.best_solution()
print(f"Best: {solution}, Fitness: {fitness:.4f}")

scipy.optimize.differential_evolution
Built into SciPy — zero extra install
Python · Production Ready

Differential Evolution is a close cousin of GA — population-based, fitness-driven, no gradients needed. Built into SciPy, so zero dependencies. Best for real-valued continuous optimisation problems.

from scipy.optimize import differential_evolution
import numpy as np

# The Rastrigin function — notoriously hard, many local minima
def rastrigin(x):
    n = len(x)
    return 10*n + sum(xi**2 - 10*np.cos(2*np.pi*xi) for xi in x)

# Define bounds for each gene
bounds = [(-5.12, 5.12)] * 10  # 10-dimensional problem

result = differential_evolution(
    rastrigin, bounds,
    strategy    = 'best1bin',  # crossover strategy
    maxiter     = 1000,
    popsize     = 15,          # pop = popsize × len(bounds)
    mutation    = (0.5, 1),
    recombination = 0.7,
    seed        = 42
)
print(f"Global min ≈ {result.fun:.6f} at {result.x}")

pymoo
Multi-Objective Optimisation Framework
Python · Multi-Objective

When you need to optimise multiple conflicting objectives simultaneously (e.g., minimise cost and maximise performance), pymoo provides state-of-the-art multi-objective algorithms including NSGA-II, NSGA-III, and MOEA/D.

from pymoo.algorithms.moo.nsga2 import NSGA2
from pymoo.core.problem import Problem
from pymoo.optimize import minimize
import numpy as np

# ZDT1: classic multi-objective benchmark
class MyProblem(Problem):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__(n_var=30, n_obj=2,
                          xl=np.zeros(30), xu=np.ones(30))
    def _evaluate(self, x, out, *args, **kwargs):
        f1 = x[:, 0]
        g  = 1 + 9/29 * np.sum(x[:, 1:], axis=1)
        f2 = g * (1 - (f1/g)**0.5)
        out["F"] = np.column_stack([f1, f2])

result = minimize(
    MyProblem(),
    NSGA2(pop_size=100),
    ('n_gen', 200),
    verbose=True
)
# result.F contains the Pareto front

The Takeaway: The entire power of a Genetic Algorithm flows from one idea — you only need to judge solutions, not derive them. Once you internalise that, you'll recognise a remarkable category of problems around you — complex, multi-constraint, combinatorial — that traditional methods struggle with, but evolution handles elegantly. That's not a coincidence. Evolution is, in a very real sense, the universe's own general-purpose optimiser.
